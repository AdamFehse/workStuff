End of 2024 Reflection

   From the Director's Desk

Dear Confluencenter Community and friends, 

I hope this message finds you in good health and high spirits. 

As we wrap up another dynamic year, we want to extend our heartfelt gratitude for your ongoing support. 2024 has been a year of growth, collaboration, and meaningful impact across the UA campus, the Tucson community, and beyond. 

This year, the Confluencenter hosted and co-sponsored numerous events, supported innovative faculty and student projects, and expanded our initiatives with the generous backing of the Mellon and Ford Foundations. 

A standout moment was the launch of Reclaiming the Border Narrative: Digital Archive Launch and Symposium, a Ford-funded collaboration with The University of Arizona Libraries Special Collections. This digital archive amplifies the voices of borderlands and immigrant communities, creating a platform for future research, advocacy, and artistic expression. We even continued the excitement by granting three southern Arizona artists and scholars to creatively repurpose the Reclaiming the Border Narrative (RBN) archive

Our renowned Show & Tell events united our campus and Tucson community in presenting interdisciplinary work within our programs, sharing innovative work, and fostering dialogue on important issues. This year, we highlighted conversations on migration from local perspectives, heard from mariachi leaders on Tucson’s mariachi legacy, and reinforced the importance of preserving our shared history and the transformative power of storytelling.

We presented virtually to our colleagues in Australia at the "Regions, Humanities, Wellbeing: The Relationship Between Humanities and Communities in the Regions" Symposium hosted in Cairns, Australia. We attended and presented at the Latina/x/o Studies Association conference hosted at Arizona State University to showcase the Fronteridades program and promote the Confluencenter’s mission.  

We strengthened our binational partnerships, notably with cultural institutions in Nogales, Sonora. In early August, we received some of the results of the Binational Arts Grants program, which supported emerging artists in Mexico, further cementing our ties with communities on both sides of the border. 

All of this work could not be done without the dedication and diligence of the Confluencenter team. 

As we reflect on a year of exciting achievements, we look forward to 2025 with great anticipation. The work we do together is more vital than ever, and we are thrilled by the opportunities to continue uplifting innovative and interdisciplinary studies on our campus and communities beyond Tucson in the year ahead.

Thank you for being part of our journey. We wish you a joyful holiday season and look forward to another year of meaningful collaboration! 

Dr. Javier Duran 

Director, Confluencenter for Creative Inquiry

Conferences & Travel
We spread the word about the work we do!
In July, CCI Director, Javier Duran presented virtually at the "Regions, Humanities, Wellbeing: The Relationship Between Humanities and Communities in the Regions" Symposium hosted in Cairns, Australia.

Dr. Duran was a keynote speaker for, “Bridging Interdisciplinarity and Borders: The Role of an Academic Humanities Center.” By using examples of the programs at the Confluencenter Dr. Duran spoke on the significance of establishing interconnectedness and building positive relationships with communities. 

CCI travelled to Tempe, AZ to present at the Latina/o/x Studies Association conference on our Fronteridades program to scholars and educators. We talked to people from schools all across the country! It was a great networking opportunity for our team to share more about the programs and resources we offer as a research institution to faculty, graduate and undergraduate students, and community members.

Reclaiming the Border Narrative
The University of Arizona’s Libraries’ Special Collections and the Confluencecenter for Creative Inquiry hosted the Reclaiming the Border Narrative Symposium. We convened over 200 stakeholders for a multidisciplinary Symposium to celebrate the culmination of the Reclaiming the Border Narrative Initiative and the launch of the accompanying Digital Archive.
This community-engaged archive centers the diversity and multidimensionality of experiences of U.S.-Mexico borderlands communities and migrants. The projects housed in this archive illuminate dignity, joy, resilience, resistance, beauty, sorrow, pain, humor, and love, while challenging and expanding beyond externally imposed and dominant harmful narratives about the region and about migration.
We made many beautiful connections in building this archive and are deeply honored to steward it for the long term. We look forward to seeing how this archive inspires scholarly engagement, archival practice, research, art, organizing, and policy advocacy in the future.

Ugly Little Monkeys: A True American Story
Over 150 people attended to watch “Ugly Little Monkeys: A True American Story”. The film documents the origins of Los Changuitos Feos de Tucson, the first youth mariachi group in the nation. It tells the story of a group of kids who grew up in the 1960s in the working-class barrios of Tucson and how they rose to national fame. After the film we had a panel with Wilfred Arvizu, David Valdez and Francisco Escobar which was moderated by Confluencenter Director, Javier Duran. 
This screening was made possible through the collaborative efforts of the University of Arizona Hispanic Serving Institutions, Latinx Staff and Faculty Association, School of Music, W.A. Franke Honors College, and the Adalberto and Ana Guerrero Center.
